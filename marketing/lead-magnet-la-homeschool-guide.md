# Lead Magnet: The LA Homeschool Community Starter Guide

**Working title:** *The LA Homeschool Community Starter Guide: 25 places to meet like-minded families (before you ever join a co-op)*

**Purpose:** Middle-of-funnel lead magnet. A genuinely useful, non-salesy PDF that solves the #1 felt problem of LA families considering homeschooling — "Where do I even meet people?" — and introduces Gadugy softly on the last page.

**Distribution:** Free download in exchange for an email (mailto to hello@gadugy.com for now, or a simple form later). Shared in Facebook groups (with admin permission), co-op newsletters, QR flyers at park days, library community boards.

**Format target:** 14–18 pages, letter size, one place per page block, lots of white space, warm photography feel (or clean icon-based design if no photos).

---

## Tone & copy rules for the whole guide

- Warm, wholesome, rooted, practical. Written like a knowledgeable neighbor, not a brand.
- Never overtly political. Values language stays gentle: "like-minded," "share your hopes," "your people."
- Every listing gets a "Good for" tag (ages, vibe) and a "First-timer tip" so the guide feels lived-in, not scraped.
- No competitor shade, no co-op shade. The point is: community comes first, structure later.
- Gadugy appears ONLY on the final page — and briefly in a one-line footer ("Brought to you by Gadugy — gadugy.com").

We've organized the guide by neighborhood cluster — Pasadena & Altadena, the South Bay, the San Fernando Valley, Glendale & Burbank, Long Beach, Santa Clarita, and the Westside — so you can start close to home. Because the best homeschool community isn't the most impressive one. It's the one you can get to on a Tuesday.

Pick one spot. Go this week. Say hi to one person.

## FULL TEXT — Chapter 1: Pasadena & Altadena (~500 words)

**Northeast LA's quiet homeschool heartland.**

If there's a part of Los Angeles where homeschool families have been quietly building community for decades, it's the foothills of the San Gabriels. Pasadena and Altadena families have an embarrassment of riches here — canyons, gardens, world-class libraries, and a culture that already expects kids to be outside learning on weekday mornings. You won't stand out. That's the point.

**Eaton Canyon Nature Center — Pasadena**
The nature center runs family-friendly programs and weekend nature walks, but the real magic is the easy, mostly-flat trail into the canyon — doable with a stroller with decent wheels, exciting enough for a seven-year-old. On weekday mornings you'll spot homeschool families by the dozen.
*Good for: ages 2–10, wiggly kids, nature-nerd parents.*
*First-timer tip: go before 10am for parking, and check the nature center's free program calendar — the docent walks are gentle and welcoming to tag-alongs.*

**Pasadena Central Library — Children's Room**
Pasadena's main library has one of the strongest children's rooms in the county, and its weekday story times draw a noticeably homeschool-heavy crowd. Linger after story time — this is where "we're thinking about it too" conversations happen naturally.
*Good for: ages 0–8, rainy days, meeting brand-new homeschoolers.*
*First-timer tip: the staff keep an informal eye on local homeschool happenings; ask at the children's desk what's coming up.*

**Altadena Main Library & the Farnsworth Park scene**
Altadena's library is smaller and scrappier, and the community around it is tight. Nearby Farnsworth Park hosts informal park gatherings where homeschool families tend to land on weekday afternoons, especially in good weather.
*Good for: ages 0–9, families who want small-and-known over big-and-polished.*
*First-timer tip: Altadena runs on word of mouth. Ask one parent at the park, and you'll leave with three more spots that aren't in any guide — including, honestly, this one.*

**Kidspace Children's Museum — Free Family Nights**
Kidspace is admission-priced, but its free family nights (check the calendar, typically monthly) turn it into a cross-city homeschool mixer. Kids run the climbing towers while parents actually get to finish a conversation.
*Good for: ages 1–10, parents who need adult conversation as much as kids need play.*
*First-timer tip: free nights get busy — arrive right at opening and start on the outdoor side, where it's easier to chat.*

**Arroyo Seco weekend wanderings**
The Arroyo — the wash and trails below the Colorado Street Bridge — is where northeast LA families go when nobody planned anything and everybody showed up anyway. Seasonal stream, shady sycamores, room to roam.
*Good for: all ages, low-structure weekends, bring-the-dog families.*
*First-timer tip: park near the Kidspace side for restrooms; the southern stretch feels wilder but facilities thin out.*

Start with one of these this week. In Pasadena and Altadena, you won't be the new family for long — this corner of LA has a long memory for friendly faces, and a short one for whether your curriculum is picked out yet.

## How to produce this guide free, in one weekend

**Saturday morning (2–3 hrs): Write & edit**
1. Copy this outline into a fresh Google Doc.
2. Write the remaining chapters using Chapter 1 as the voice template — 3–4 places each, same structure (name, what it is, "Good for," first-timer tip).
3. Read aloud once. Cut anything that sounds like marketing.

**Saturday afternoon (1–2 hrs): Format in Google Docs**
1. Page setup: letter size, 1" margins.
2. Fonts: use one friendly serif or rounded sans for headings (e.g. Georgia, Quicksand via Docs fonts) and a clean body font (e.g. Lato, Arial). Two fonts max.
3. Chapter titles as Heading 1 so Docs auto-builds a table of contents.
4. One place per block, generous white space. Insert a page break before each chapter.
5. Add the footer: "The LA Homeschool Community Starter Guide • gadugy.com"

**Sunday morning (1–2 hrs): Cover & polish in Canva (free tier)**
1. Canva → "Presentation (16:9)" or "A4 Document" → design ONE cover page only.
2. Search Canva free photos for "family hike," "kids garden," or "Los Angeles trail" — pick something warm and unposed, or use a simple illustrated-map style from free elements.
3. Title in big friendly type, subtitle small, "Free guide • gadugy.com" at the bottom.
4. Export cover as PNG.
5. Back in Google Docs: Insert → Image at the top of page 1, full width, title page done. Add the QR code to gadugy.com on the last page (free generator: qr-code-generator.com or Canva's built-in QR app).

**Sunday afternoon (30 min): Export & ship**
1. File → Download → PDF.
2. Name it: `LA-Homeschool-Community-Starter-Guide.pdf`
3. Host it: attach to the Gadugy waitlist confirmation email, or upload to a free Google Drive link / a /guide page on gadugy.com.
4. Test the whole path yourself: find link → open PDF → read page 1 → click through to gadugy.com.

**Total cost: $0. Total time: one weekend.**
